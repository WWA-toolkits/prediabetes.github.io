What is Prediabetes, Really?

It simply means your blood sugar levels are higher than normal, but not yet high enough to be diagnosed as type 2 diabetes. Think of it as a yellow light‚Äîa signal to slow down and pay attention.

The Key Player: Insulin Resistance

Your body isn't using insulin as effectively as it should. Insulin is a hormone that acts like a key, letting sugar from your blood into your cells for energy. When cells become "resistant," more sugar stays in your bloodstream, leading to higher A1C levels.

Know Your Numbers

The most important number to understand is your Hemoglobin A1C (or just "A1C"). It gives you a 3-month average of your blood sugar levels. It's the best indicator of your progress.

Questions for Your Doctor

Use your next appointment to build your support team. Here are some great questions to ask:

Testing Frequency

"Based on my numbers, how often should we re-check my A1C?"

Medication

"At what point should we consider medication like Metformin?"

Referrals

"Can you refer me to a registered dietitian or a certified diabetes educator?"

Other Factors

"Are there any other factors like blood pressure or cholesterol we need to watch closely?"

Movement is Medicine

Physical activity makes your cells much more sensitive to insulin, which is exactly what we want! It helps your body use sugar for energy instead of letting it build up in your blood.

The goal isn't intense workouts. It's just moving more than you did yesterday.

The Power of a 10-Minute Walk

Studies show that a simple 10-15 minute walk after your biggest meal of the day can significantly lower your blood sugar for hours.

Your First Goal: 150 Minutes a Week

That's the magic number recommended by the CDC. It sounds like a lot, but look how it breaks down:

  • 22 minutes every day
  • 30 minutes, 5 days a week
  • Two 15-minute walks, 5 days a week

Managing your health is easier and more effective when you have support. This can come from family, friends, or health professionals.

Find a "Health Buddy"

Is there someone else you know working on their health? Team up to share wins and challenges.

Ask for Professional Help

Stress & Sleep Matter, Too

Chronic stress and poor sleep can raise blood sugar just like food can. They are just as important to manage.

Simple Steps for Better Rest & Less Stress:

  • Consistent Bedtime: Aim for 7-8 hours of sleep per night.
  • Screen-Free Hour: Put away phones and screens an hour before bed.
  • 5-Minute Breathers: When you feel stressed, take 5 deep, slow breaths.
  • Mindful Moments: Find one small moment of joy or gratitude each day.
